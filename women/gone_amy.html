Gone Amy

Gone Amy is the perfect woman, and that is exactly the problem. On the surface everything is impeccable — crisp button-downs tucked into tailored chinos, a pretty wrap dress in dusty blue, a neat cardigan over a silk camisole, ballet flats, a ponytail so smooth it looks considered. It is the aesthetic of a certain kind of accomplished, composed American femininity: preppy but not showy, pretty but not trying, put-together in a way that looks effortless because enormous effort has been applied to make it look that way. The palette is muted and cool — clinical whites, dusty rose, sage green, warm camel, soft taupe — punctuated occasionally by something that cuts a little sharper: oxblood, cold steel grey, deep navy. Nothing is ever warm enough to be entirely comforting. The mood is a beautiful house that is too quiet. Natural light that is flat rather than golden. A kitchen counter that is too clean. Everything is in its place, which is somehow the most unsettling thing of all. The power here is entirely invisible and absolutely total — she is the most dangerous woman in the room and she is thinking, apparently, about nothing at all.
