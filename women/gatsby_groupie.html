Gatsby Groupie

Gatsby Groupie is the East Egg set at the height of the Jazz Age — not the exuberant flapper in the speakeasy but the languid, knowing woman draped across a chaise in Gatsby's mansion, cocktail in hand, watching everything with cool and calculating eyes. The silhouette is distinctly 1920s: dropped waists, fluid columns, hemlines grazing the knee — shapes that feel revolutionary without announcing themselves. Embellishment is fine and considered rather than exuberant: intricate Art Deco beading in geometric patterns, delicate fringe that shimmers with the slightest movement, silk charmeuse that catches the light without demanding it. The palette runs from champagne and ivory through midnight blue and deep emerald to classic black and antique gold — all the colors of a candlelit ballroom at two in the morning. Accessories are few but perfect: a single rope of pearls, elbow-length gloves, a jeweled headband worn low on the forehead. The power here is never loud. It lives in a raised eyebrow, a perfectly timed glance across the room, the quiet confidence of a woman who knows that everyone in the room is watching her and has decided she doesn't particularly care.
