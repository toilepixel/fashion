Relic Hunter

Relic Hunter is the man who didn't dress for adventure — he just never stopped having one. His wardrobe is built entirely from necessity and has acquired its character through use rather than intention: a battered leather jacket creased at every flex point, canvas trousers worn soft at the knee, linen shirts with the sleeves rolled to the elbow, boots that have seen actual mud and dried in actual sun. Nothing is precious. Everything has a story. The silhouette is easy and functional — nothing restricts movement, nothing is tailored beyond what's useful, layers exist because the temperature changes between the jungle floor and the mountain pass. The palette is all earth and heat and dust: warm tan, khaki, faded olive, worn brown, sun-bleached cream, rust, dark sand — colors that have been outdoors so long they've become part of the landscape. Hardware is utilitarian: buckles, brass, canvas straps. The mood is a map spread on a wooden table, a rope bridge over something you'd rather not think about, late-afternoon light in a place most people have never heard of. He is not performing adventure. He is simply in the middle of one.
